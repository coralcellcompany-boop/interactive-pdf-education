Computed axial tomography (CT)
Introduction
• Synonym of computed axial tomography.
• Provides us with slices (tomograms) of the body.
• Axial plane is the most commonly used plane in CT.
• Gives us 2D images
How does CT work?
▪ CT uses X-rays to produce an image. The same basic principles apply to CT as to
all other X-ray studies. X-rays are blocked (attenuated) by tissues depending upon
their density (atomic number). Air is black on CT and minerals are white.
▪ To obtain a slice, an X-ray source rotates around the body in an arc while an X-ray
detection source rotates opposite the source on the opposite side of the body. The
computer analyzes the number and density of the transmitted X-rays, calculates
the coordinates, and assigns a gray scale to individual picture elements (pixels) that
will make the final picture.

Contrast
A special dye called contrast material is needed for some CT scans to help highlight
the areas of your body being examined. The contrast material blocks X-rays and
appears white on images, which can help emphasize blood vessels, intestines or
other structures
What is it used for?
1. In contrast CT, a fluid containing iodine (mineral density) is injected
intravenously.
2. Contrast is routinely employed during chest, abdomen, and pelvis CT.
3. It helps to identify vascular structures so that they may be differentiated from
other normal or abnormal structures.
4. Contrast also helps to characterize certain types of pathology, such as
infection or vascular malformations.
5. It is not necessary to specify on the order whether you would like contrast to
be administered.
6. The exception is head CT, for which you do need to specify contrast.
7. Contrast is indicated if you suspect primary or metastatic cancer, infection
(abscess), vascular malformation, or aneurysm.
How is it given to the patient?
1. By mouth: If your esophagus or stomach is being scanned, you may need to
swallow a liquid that contains contrast material. This may taste unpleasant.
2. By injection: Contrast agents can be injected through a vein in your arm to
help your gallbladder, urinary tract, liver or blood vessels stand out on the
images. You may experience a feeling of warmth during the injection or a
metallic taste in your mouth.

Side effects of Contrast
1. Most reactions are mild and result in a rash or itchiness. In rare instances, an
allergic reaction can be serious, even life-threatening.
2. If you were given contrast material, you may receive special instructions. In
some cases, you may be asked to wait for a short time before leaving to ensure
that you feel well after the exam. After the scan, you'll likely be told to drink
lots of fluids to help your kidneys remove the contrast material from your body
Exceptions where contrast is usually not used:
1. CT KUBs (looking for renal stones)
2. CT heads (unless a mass lesion is suspected)
3. Poor renal function (eGFR <30) – non-contrast CT or alternative modalities
used
Risks of contrast administration:
1. Contrast-induced nephropathy – Creatinine increasing 25% within 3 days,
with no other apparent reason. Usually self-limiting but can cause
complications of kidney disease. Incidence of ~2% in patients without risk
factors (e.g. diabetes)
2. Anaphylaxis (immediate, within 1 hour)
3. Allergy (delayed, up to 7 days)

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)
Mechanism of action
▪ Getting an image from MRI depends upon the presence of protons in the body.
▪ Protons are free hydrogen atoms (proton without electrons). They are abundant
in the body. With their net positive charge, protons are small magnets, each
having a north and a south pole.
▪ When placed in a strong magnetic field, enough of these tiny proton magnets
align to form a single magnetic vector. A radiofrequency pulse is added to this
steady state and tips the net magnetic vector off axis.
▪ As the vector returns to its alignment in the magnet, energy is released. It is this
energy that is used to create the image.
What is being imaged with MRI?
▪ In general, MRI is water imaging.
▪ Most pathologic conditions in the body are associated with edema, or water

How does MR work?
MRI uses a strong magnetic field and radio waves to create detailed images by
aligning the body's hydrogen protons, causing them to spin.
When a radiofrequency pulse is applied, the protons are knocked out of alignment
and then release energy as they realign with the magnetic field.
This released energy is measured by the MRI machine's coils, and different tissues
return to their original state at different rates, which allows the system to
differentiate between them and create an image.
Protons (nuclei of hydrogen atoms) placed in a strong magnetic field are excited by
a radio-frequency pulse, then allowed to loose energy.
The loss of gained energy occurs either to the surrounding matrix (T1) or through
interaction between each other (T2).
The loss of energy is measured and used to produce images.
Advantages of MR
1. No ionizing radiation.
2. High contrast resolution.

Aortic dissection is the most common cause of
